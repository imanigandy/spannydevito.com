The origin story

He was born in a puppy mill in Missouri. It gets worse from there.

On May 22, we adopted a cocker spaniel puppy mill survivor formerly known as Keller through Rocky Mountain Cocker Rescue. We took one look at him—scrappy, suspicious, clearly plotting something, built like a little ottoman with legs—and named him Spanny DeVito. It was the only correct choice.

Six days later, he was in the emergency vet.

I immediately decided to burn my house down. Portia said I was overreacting. She was probably right. Probably.

Spanny was hospitalized with Acute Hemorrhagic Diarrhea Syndrome (AHDS)—a sudden, life-threatening condition that causes severe dehydration and requires immediate IV fluid intervention. The emergency vet's first concern when he came in was parvo. It was not parvo. When his PCR panel came back, he tested positive for giardia. His treating veterinarian noted in a signed clinical addendum that he was "likely to have been infected prior to adoption or during transport."

He spent two days at the Emergency Pet Clinic of Longmont on IV fluids. He is home now. He is recovering. He is also currently confined to a pen and crate while we wait for a clean fecal test, which means he spends a lot of time looking at us with an expression that says he knows exactly what we did and he has not forgiven us.

In his first week home, we incurred nearly $3,000 in emergency veterinary expenses. We adopted Spanny fully expecting to take responsibility for his long-term care. What we did not expect was a medical emergency requiring hospitalization within days of bringing him home—for an infection his own vet said he likely had before he left their care.

What the rescue said

We asked for help. Here is what they told us.

We reached out to Rocky Mountain Cocker Rescue and documented everything: the timeline, the PCR results, the vet's clinical finding, the incubation period. We asked them to share in the cost of emergency care for a dog who their own treating veterinarian said was likely infected before he came to us.

Their Medical Director's first response, sent while Spanny was still hospitalized:

"It would have been a better idea to take him to your vet than the ER for diarrhea." — Suzette Compton, RMCR Medical Director, May 30, 2026

For context: the emergency veterinarian who treated Spanny documented that his condition was life-threatening and required immediate IV fluid intervention. Waiting would have risked fatal hypovolemic shock. But sure.

After two weeks of emails, the rescue's president responded. Among other things, she suggested Spanny may have contracted giardia at a dog park. He had been in our home and backyard for six days. The ER intake record—which the rescue had access to—documents under dog parks and boarding: No.

She also offered this explanation for why a single dog in Spanny's transport cohort had diarrhea upon arrival but it wasn't a concern:

"Only one reported their foster dog had diarrhea upon arrival, but thankfully, it was resolved with plain yogurt and pumpkin, in just a few days." — Audra Bowen, RMCR President, June 11, 2026

Spanny's condition required hospitalization, IV fluids, antibiotics, and a PCR panel. But yogurt and pumpkin is a reasonable comparison, we guess.

The rescue declined to contribute anything toward his care. They also did not offer to refund the adoption fee.

We are not asking you to be angry on our behalf. We are telling you what happened because people who adopt from rescues deserve to know what accountability looks like—or doesn't—when something goes wrong.
